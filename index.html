Prototype decision-support tool for exploring steel-supply trade-offs in cost, carbon, and sourcing assumptions across locations and time.

Variables Included in This Prototype

  • Region-specific base steel cost
  • Region-specific CO₂ intensity
  • Delivered cost (cost + tariffs + shipping − incentives)
  • Carbon price and carbon-adjusted cost
  • Projected volumes by region (bubble size)
  • Timeline from 2025 to 2050
  • Domestic vs imported sourcing effects (U.S. vs rest of world)

Gapminder-Style Cost–Carbon Bubble Plot

Each bubble is a region in a given year. The horizontal axis is delivered cost ($/ton), the vertical axis is CO₂ intensity (kg CO₂/ton), and bubble size reflects projected volume. Use the time slider and play/pause controls to see how relative cost–carbon positions evolve over time under different tariffs, shipping costs, incentives, and carbon prices.

Summary: U.S. vs China (Current Scenario at Start Year)

Metric U.S. China Difference Meaning
Delivered Cost Difference in total landed steel cost per ton. Positive means U.S. steel is more expensive than Chinese steel in this scenario; negative means U.S. is cheaper.
CO₂ Intensity Difference in emissions per ton of steel. Positive means U.S. steel emits more CO₂ per ton; negative means U.S. steel is cleaner per ton than Chinese steel.
Carbon-Adjusted Cost Delivered cost plus the monetized value of emissions using the selected carbon price. Positive difference means U.S. steel is more expensive after accounting for carbon; negative difference means U.S. is cheaper on a carbon-adjusted basis.

Next Steps to a Robust Decision-Support Tool

To turn this prototype into a full production decision-support tool, the next steps are to replace stylized values with real market and emissions data, track how key variables evolve over time, quantify volatility and uncertainty, and use dimensionality-reduction methods - like PCA (Principle Component Analysis) - to identify the factors that matter most. This creates a defensible foundation for stress-testing procurement strategies under changing market conditions and guiding sourcing decisions with clearer insight. The most achievabale actionable step is to start with a handful of variables you have data for, gather evidence over several months, and iteratively grow the model with more variables.
